Discussion 1: The Fourth Industrial Revolution

This three-week discussion focused on the impact of Industry 4.0 and Industry 5.0 within a sector of professional interest. I explored this topic through the lens of healthcare and emergency medical services, linking digital transformation to machine learning, resilience, and socio-technical risk.

Initial Post

In my initial post, I discussed how the transition from Industry 4.0 to Industry 5.0 has transformed healthcare and emergency medical services through increased automation, machine learning, and interconnected digital systems. I used the 2017 WannaCry ransomware attack on the UK National Health Service as a real-world example of information system failure in a high-risk environment.

“In critical sectors such as healthcare, resilience must be embedded as a core design principle rather than treated as a secondary feature.”

My discussion argued that although Industry 4.0 improves efficiency through integration and intelligent systems, it can also increase fragility when resilience is not built into infrastructure. I then linked this to Industry 5.0, which places stronger emphasis on human-centred design, resilience, transparency, and safe failure behaviour.

Peer Responses

In my response to Mosleh’s post on the CrowdStrike incident, I extended the discussion by focusing on cascading failures, tightly coupled infrastructures, staged deployment, rollback mechanisms, and architectural resilience. I argued that digital systems should be designed not only for efficiency, but also for controlled failure and recovery.

In my response to Ioanna’s post, I developed the idea that machine learning systems in dynamic and high-risk environments should not be evaluated only by accuracy. I highlighted the need for robustness under uncertainty, safe degradation, interpretability, and effective human oversight when systems are disrupted.

Summary Post

In my summary post, I reflected on how the discussion changed my understanding of Industry 5.0. I concluded that the transition from Industry 4.0 to Industry 5.0 is not simply about advancing technology, but about correcting the limitations of efficiency-driven systems by embedding resilience, human-centred design, and responsible governance.

This discussion strengthened my understanding that machine learning systems must be assessed within the wider socio-technical ecosystems in which they operate. In healthcare and emergency services, this is especially important because system failure can directly affect patient safety and service continuity.

Evidence included: Initial post, two peer responses, and summary post for Collaborative Discussion 1: The Fourth Industrial Revolution.

Discussion 2: Legal and Ethical Views on ANN Applications

This discussion explored the risks and benefits of AI writers, based on Hutson’s (2021) article Robo-writers: The rise and risks of language-generating AI. The discussion encouraged critical reflection on reliability, hallucination, bias, authorship, accountability, and human oversight in language-generating systems.

Initial Post

In my initial post, I argued that AI writers are highly useful in structured administrative tasks such as drafting emails, summaries, and reports, where efficiency and consistency are valuable. However, I also argued that these benefits are context-dependent and do not transfer safely into more complex or high-stakes domains.

“AI writers should be understood as assistive technologies rather than autonomous systems.”

I examined key limitations of AI writers, including hallucination, lack of genuine understanding, bias, and the risk of over-reliance. I also discussed ethical concerns surrounding authorship, originality, and intellectual responsibility. My conclusion was that AI writers should be treated as assistive technologies rather than autonomous systems, and that their responsible use depends on critical human judgment, transparency, and governance.

Peer Responses

In my response to Monique, I extended the discussion by focusing on the difference between fluent text and genuine understanding. I highlighted the danger that AI-generated content may appear convincing while remaining inaccurate, biased, or conceptually weak. I also discussed the need for dataset documentation, model reporting, and broader governance mechanisms beyond simple human review.

In my response to Mansour, I developed the distinction between useful assistance and safe delegation. I argued that AI writers may help with drafting and summarisation, but this does not mean they can be trusted autonomously. I linked this to hallucination, transparency, bias, and the educational risk of weakening originality and critical thinking when users rely too heavily on generated content.

Summary Post

In my summary post, I reflected that the central issue is not simply whether AI writers are useful, but under what conditions they can be trusted. The discussion helped me move beyond seeing hallucinations as isolated technical errors and toward understanding them as part of a wider governance and accountability challenge.

I concluded that AI writers should be understood as assistive rather than autonomous systems. Their use must be guided by human oversight, transparency, disclosure, and a clear awareness of both their technical limitations and their societal implications.

Evidence included: Initial post, two peer responses, and summary post for Collaborative Discussion 2: Legal and Ethical Views on ANN Applications.

Reflection on Discussion-Based Learning

These discussions were valuable because they developed a different kind of learning from the practical coding activities in the module. They required me to read critically, engage with academic literature, respond constructively to peers, and connect technical machine learning concepts to wider ethical, social, and professional issues.

They also strengthened my ability to write analytically, build arguments with evidence, and refine my thinking through dialogue. This was especially important in areas such as healthcare AI and AI-generated language, where technical performance alone is not sufficient to determine whether a system is safe, responsible, or appropriate for real-world use.
